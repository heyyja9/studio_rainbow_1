Brand History

The idea for Rainbow Loom was conceived in 2010 by Cheong Choon Ng, a Malaysian immigrant and mechanical engineer working for Nissan. Inspired by his daughters, Teresa and Michelle, making rubber band bracelets, and hindered by his larger fingers, Ng built a wooden push-pin board to improve his dexterity. Although the initial, bulkier design didn't win his daughters over, he refined it by adding more rows of push pins, eventually capturing their interest.

Teresa, his oldest daughter, later suggested he manufacture and sell the loom, and his brother Cheong Yeow Ng encouraged him to market it online. Leveraging his background in product design, quality control, and manufacturing, Ng and his family invested $10 000 in Chinese manufacturers, dividing $5000 for molding the template, and $5000 for the kit's parts. In the summer of 2011, a 2000 pound shipment of rubber bands arrived, and Ng began assembling the kits at night after work, while his wife, Tyng Fen Chan, worked on them during the day.

After early sales were hampered by customer confusion over the innovative toy, Ng and his daughters began posting instructional YouTube videos to educate potential buyers. These videos proved pivotal, as in summer 2012, a Learning Express Toys store (a chain of 130 franchises) ordered 24 looms, and called to reorder just two days later. Soon, other locations rushed to stock up, and by summer 2013, Rainbow Loom was listed as one of the chain's top toys.

As of August 2013, Ng manages a team of 12 from a 7500-square-foot warehouse near his home, handling distribution while manufacturing occurs in China. In all, Rainbow Loom is carried by 600 retailers, with sales exceeding just over one million units at retail prices between $15 and $17.

Source: The New York Times

The Bracelet Craze

The owner of a Learning Express Toys in Chattanooga, Mary Beth Conklin, states that Rainbow Loom's popularity has exceeded that of past hits like Webkinz and Silly Bandz.

Conklin, whose store began selling Rainbow Loom kits in October 2012, shares that “Rainbow Loom far exceeds those trends. We've been in business five years and we've never carried anything as popular.” She believes that the product has become a social phenomenon because “kids love trading the bracelets. [...] [They] find great satisfaction in being challenged by the endless number of designs they can make.” She goes on to explain how parents have even hopped on the trend as “it's educational and enhances fine motor skills, [...] [and] gets kids off the iPads, iTouches, and all things electronic.”

Similarly, Jeff Underwood, a district manager for the crafts store Michaels, mentions that the Rainbow Loom kits have been highly popular at their locations. He says, “The first day that we introduced the Rainbow Loom, we had more than 20 Michaels sold out immediately.”
